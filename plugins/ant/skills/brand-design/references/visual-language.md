# Visual Language

## Composition

The `(ant)` visual system is editorial, sharp, and typographic.

Use:

- strong black/white fields with mint as a decisive accent;
- large type blocks with compact supporting copy;
- horizontal rules and hard dividers;
- asymmetric but controlled layouts;
- generous whitespace around key statements;
- direct crops of real work, people, or device/mockup imagery;
- bracketed micro-labels that feel like authored annotations.

Avoid:

- generic gradient hero sections;
- glassmorphism, glow-heavy UI, soft neumorphic cards, or decorative blobs;
- pastel-only compositions;
- crowded dashboards that lose the brand's typographic hierarchy;
- over-rounded components that make the identity feel generic;
- stock-looking imagery that does not show people, work, product, or craft.

## Typographic Behavior

Headlines should feel designed, not merely enlarged:

- use short lines and strong rhythm;
- break lines intentionally;
- use black/white inversion for emphasis;
- let mint carry one or two important words, not every keyword;
- combine large headlines with small functional labels or rules.

Body text should stay practical and scannable:

- use Inter or the existing UI body font;
- keep paragraphs compact;
- use clear labels, metadata, and direct CTAs.

## Bracket Language

The brand uses parenthesized short labels and words as visual shorthand: `(ant)`, `(WOW)`, `(WHY)`, `(HOT)`, `(FUN)`, `(NEW)`, `(WTF)`.

Use these as:

- section markers;
- small label strips;
- campaign emphasis;
- empty-state or onboarding moments;
- social/deck annotation elements.

Do not use them as a replacement for accessible labels in controls. Real UI controls still need clear names.

## Labels

Label assets show black rectangular sticker-like marks with white type, often scattered or angled in expressive contexts. In UI work, translate that language carefully:

- use black pill/strip labels for emphasis, not every status;
- prefer hard rectangular strips over soft badges;
- keep rotation/scatter to campaign or editorial surfaces;
- avoid rotation in dense product UI where it hurts scanning;
- keep labels short, usually one word or a short bracketed phrase.

## Imagery

Use imagery when it reveals actual brand context:

- portraits on dark backgrounds for team/author surfaces;
- device/mockup scenes for digital work;
- stationery and print mockups for case studies;
- meet/background assets only when creating meeting/social/internal communication outputs.

Do not use decorative image cards when the image does not support the workflow.

## Copy Tone

The export has limited explicit Tone of Voice content. Inference from visible materials:

- direct and confident;
- craft-oriented;
- concise;
- willing to use playful bracketed emphasis;
- Czech/English mix appears in brand devices such as `(ant) crafted`, `(WOW)`, `(PROCO)`/`(PROC)` style emphasis.

When copy accuracy matters, say that the Tone of Voice is inferred from visual examples and ask for a source copy guide if available.
